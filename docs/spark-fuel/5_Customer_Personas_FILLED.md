# CUSTOMER PERSONAS — Worksheet 6

**Founder Name:** Waiz Khan
**Venture Name:** Iris Health
**Venture Description:** Iris is an AI powered heart failure care agent providing 24/7 monitoring and medication optimization. Every clinical decision comes from deterministic Python tools following AHA/ACC guidelines. The language model only extracts patient info and communicates tool outputs. Iris monitors weight trends, optimizes GDMT medications, detects adherence barriers, predicts decompensation, and escalates to clinicians.

## Persona 1: The Heart Failure Patient

**Name:** Margaret ("Maggie") Thompson
**Age:** 72
**Location:** East Baltimore, Maryland
**Occupation:** Retired school cafeteria worker

### Demographics
- African American female, widowed, lives alone in a row home
- Medicare with a Medigap supplemental plan
- Fixed income: Social Security + small pension (~$2,100/month)
- High school education
- Has an adult daughter who lives 45 minutes away and visits on weekends
- Owns a basic smartphone (gifted by daughter) but rarely uses apps

### Clinical Profile
- Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF), EF 30%, NYHA Class III
- Diagnosed 3 years ago after hospitalization for acute decompensation
- Comorbidities: Type 2 diabetes, hypertension, mild chronic kidney disease (eGFR 45)
- Current medications: furosemide 40mg daily, carvedilol 12.5mg twice daily, lisinopril 10mg daily, metformin 500mg twice daily
- Hospitalized twice in the past 12 months for fluid overload
- Sees cardiologist every 4 months at Johns Hopkins outpatient clinic

### Psychographics
- Values independence fiercely — does not want to move in with her daughter or go to assisted living
- Trusts her cardiologist but feels rushed during 15 minute appointments
- Worries about being a burden on her family
- Spiritual — attends church every Sunday, church community is her main social support
- Watches daytime TV and talks on the phone with friends daily
- Uncomfortable with technology but comfortable talking — she is a natural conversationalist

### Pain Points
1. Forgets whether she took her medications, especially the evening dose
2. Does not understand why she takes so many pills or what each one does
3. Gained 4 pounds over a week last fall but did not know it was dangerous until she ended up in the ER
4. Cannot afford the copay for one of her medications and sometimes skips it
5. Feels anxious between appointments — "What if something is wrong and I don't know?"
6. The patient portal her hospital uses is confusing — she has never logged in

### Goals
- Stay in her own home as long as possible
- Avoid going back to the hospital
- Understand her health in simple terms
- Feel like someone is watching out for her between visits

### How Iris Helps Maggie
- Voice first check ins every morning — Maggie just talks, no typing or app navigation
- Iris catches her 2 lb weight gain on day 3 and recommends a diuretic adjustment before it becomes an ER visit
- Barrier Planner identifies her medication cost issue and suggests a generic alternative on her formulary
- Plain language explanations: "Your water pill helps your heart pump better by getting rid of extra fluid"
- Her daughter gets a family view link showing Maggie's status without clinical jargon

### Quote
"I just want someone to tell me if something's wrong before I end up back in that hospital."

## Persona 2: The Heart Failure Clinic Nurse Manager

**Name:** Jennifer ("Jen") Kowalski, RN, BSN
**Age:** 38
**Location:** Baltimore, Maryland
**Occupation:** Nurse Manager, Heart Failure Bridge Clinic at a large academic medical center

### Demographics
- White female, married, two children (ages 6 and 9)
- BSN from University of Maryland, 14 years of nursing experience, 7 in heart failure
- Manages a panel of 180 HF patients with a team of 2 NPs and 1 RN
- Salary: ~$95,000/year
- Works 7 AM to 4 PM but frequently stays late charting and returning patient calls

### Professional Profile
- Responsible for post discharge follow up, medication reconciliation, and RPM oversight
- Currently uses a basic RPM platform that sends weight and BP alerts from Bluetooth scales
- Reviews 40 to 60 alerts per day, most of which are false positives (scale errors, missed readings)
- Coordinates with 4 cardiologists who rotate through the clinic
- Tracks readmission rates — her clinic's 30 day HF readmission rate is 18% (national average ~22%)

### Psychographics
- Deeply committed to her patients — knows many by name and family situation
- Frustrated by how much time she spends on administrative work vs actual patient care
- Skeptical of technology promises — has seen multiple "solutions" come and go
- Pragmatic — she needs tools that work within her existing workflow, not add another system
- Respects evidence — if you show her data, she will listen

### Pain Points
1. Alert fatigue: 70% of RPM alerts are clinically insignificant, but she must review each one
2. Phone tag: spends 2+ hours per day trying to reach patients who do not answer
3. Documentation burden: every patient interaction requires charting in Epic, often done after hours
4. No clinical reasoning in alerts: "Patient weight is 185 lbs" tells her nothing without context
5. Patients call with questions she has already answered — they forget verbal instructions
6. Cannot identify which patients are actually deteriorating vs having a bad scale day

### Goals
- Reduce time spent on false positive alerts so she can focus on patients who truly need attention
- Lower the clinic's readmission rate below 15%
- Leave work on time at least 3 days per week
- Have confidence that her sickest patients are being monitored even when the clinic is closed

### How Iris Helps Jen
- Action Packets replace raw alerts — Jen sees "Patient gained 2.3 lbs over 5 days, potassium 4.1, eGFR stable at 52. Recommend: increase furosemide 40mg to 80mg per AHA 7.3.2. Confidence: high."
- Escalation Manager only flags patients who genuinely need clinician review — estimated 80% reduction in noise
- Iris handles routine patient questions between visits (medication timing, diet reminders, symptom context)
- Clinician dashboard shows the full reasoning chain — Jen can approve, modify, or override any recommendation
- RPM billing support: Iris activities qualify for CPT 99457/99458, generating revenue for the clinic

### Quote
"I don't need another alert. I need something that tells me which patients to worry about and why."

## Persona 3: The Hospital Chief Medical Officer

**Name:** Dr. Robert ("Rob") Patel, MD, MBA
**Age:** 54
**Location:** Washington, D.C. metro area
**Occupation:** Chief Medical Officer, 450 bed community hospital in a large health system

### Demographics
- Indian American male, married, three adult children
- MD from Georgetown, MBA from Wharton, board certified in internal medicine
- 20 years of clinical practice before moving to administration
- Salary: ~$450,000/year
- Reports to the hospital CEO and health system board

### Professional Profile
- Oversees clinical quality, patient safety, and regulatory compliance for the hospital
- Directly responsible for CMS penalty performance — hospital currently pays ~$800,000/year in HRRP penalties, with HF as the largest contributor
- Manages a $4M annual budget for quality improvement initiatives
- Has approved 3 digital health pilots in the past 2 years — 1 showed results, 2 were abandoned
- Chairs the hospital's Clinical Technology Committee

### Psychographics
- Data driven and financially minded — the MBA trained him to think in ROI
- Burned by overpromising health tech vendors — now requires evidence before piloting
- Politically savvy — needs buy in from department chiefs before adopting new technology
- Understands the nursing shortage is the biggest operational threat to his hospital
- Personally believes AI will transform healthcare but is cautious about liability
- Values relationships with physicians — will not override a department chief's objection

### Pain Points
1. CMS HRRP penalties: $800K/year and growing — the board wants this number down
2. HF readmission rate of 24% — above national average, the quality committee is watching
3. Nursing shortage: 15% vacancy rate, cannot hire enough RPM nurses
4. Previous digital health investments have not demonstrated clear ROI
5. Physicians resist new technology unless it reduces their workload (not adds to it)
6. Regulatory and liability concerns about AI making clinical recommendations
7. Procurement process takes 6 to 12 months — by the time something is approved, the vendor may have changed

### Goals
- Reduce HRRP penalties by at least 50% within 2 years
- Find scalable solutions that do not require hiring more staff
- Demonstrate innovation leadership to the health system board
- Manage liability risk with any AI clinical tool

### How Iris Helps Dr. Patel
- Direct ROI case: at $75/patient/month for 200 HF patients = $180K/year. Preventing just 12 readmissions saves $180K to $300K in direct costs plus reduces CMS penalties
- Does not require hiring nurses — Iris automates the clinical reasoning that RPM nurses currently perform
- Deterministic, auditable architecture satisfies his liability concerns — every recommendation traces to a specific guideline, not an AI "black box"
- Pilot structure: start with 50 patients over 6 months, measure readmission reduction, then scale
- FHIR R4 export demonstrates EHR integration readiness — his IT team will want to see this
- Action Packets give his cardiologists full transparency and override capability — physician adoption barrier is lowered

### Quote
"Show me the data on readmission reduction and I'll find the budget. But I need to know exactly how this thing makes its decisions — I'm not putting my hospital's name behind a black box."
